Philosophical & Methodological Framework Map

AI Adoption in Judicial Systems: A Comparative Study

Available Option
Selected for This Research
1
ONTOLOGY
What is the nature of reality? What exists?
Realism
Reality exists independently of our perception. Single objective truth.
Relativism
Multiple realities exist, constructed by individuals and groups.
Critical Realism
Reality exists but is stratified (empirical, actual, real). Knowledge is partial and fallible.
Pragmatism
Reality is what works in practice. Truth is provisional.
Why Critical Realism?
The phenomenon has a dual nature: AI as technology (measurable, objective) AND institutional adoption (complex, context-dependent). Critical Realism acknowledges both layers and seeks underlying causal mechanisms. It also supports moving from description → explanation → evaluation → recommendations.
2
EPISTEMOLOGY
What counts as knowledge? How can we know?
Positivism
Only observable, measurable facts. Value-free, objective knowledge.
Interpretivism
Understanding through interpretation of meanings, context-dependent.
Pragmatism
Knowledge is what works. Problem-centered approach.
Critical Realist Epistemology
Knowledge is fallible but possible. We infer mechanisms from their effects.
Types of Knowledge This Research Will Produce:
Pragmatic/Empirical — What actually worked?
Normative/Rights-Based — What aligns with principles?
Balancing — How to balance competing values?
3
AXIOLOGY
What is the role of values in research?
Value-Free
Research should be objective and detached from values.
Value-Laden (Explicit)
Values are acknowledged and made transparent. Evaluative stance is justified.
Value-Driven
Research explicitly aims to promote specific values or change.
Position
This research is explicitly evaluative and normative. The MCDA criteria embody value choices (what makes "good" AI adoption?). These choices are transparent and theoretically justified, not hidden behind false objectivity.
4
LOGIC OF INFERENCE
How do we move from evidence to conclusions?
Deduction
From theory → hypothesis → testing → confirmation/rejection.
Induction
From observations → patterns → theory building.
Abduction
Inference to the best explanation.
Retroduction (Cyclical)
Combining induction, retroduction, and deduction in iterative cycles.
The Cyclical Process:
1. Induction — Observe cases, identify patterns
2. Retroduction — What mechanisms could explain this?
3. Deduction — Test explanations on other cases
5
THEORETICAL PERSPECTIVE
What theoretical lens shapes the analysis?
Formalism (Legal)
Law as autonomous, logical system of rules.
Legal Realism
Law in practice ≠ law in books. Focus on what courts actually do.
Law & Economics
Legal rules analyzed through efficiency lens.
Critical Legal Studies
Law as instrument of power and ideology.
Supporting Theories:
Diffusion of Innovations (Rogers)
Institutional Theory (North)
Implementation Science (Fixsen et al.)
6
RESEARCH STRATEGY
What is the overall approach to inquiry?
Single Case Study
In-depth analysis of one case.
Multiple Case Studies
Comparing several cases in depth.
Large-N Survey
Broad coverage of many cases.
Nested Analysis
Large-N survey THEN selected in-depth cases. Best of both worlds.
Two-Level Design:
Level 1: MCDA on 10 countries (patterns, classification)
Level 2: 3 in-depth cases (mechanisms, CMO configurations)
7
POSITION ON GENERALIZATION
Can findings be applied beyond the studied cases?
Statistical Generalization
From sample to population with probability.
No Generalization
Findings are unique to the cases studied.
Contingent Generalization
"In contexts where X, Y, Z are present, this practice is likely to work."
Analytical Generalization
Generalizing to theory, not to populations.
The Goal
Build a Middle-Range Theory — not universal laws, not just case descriptions, but context-sensitive guidance: "Countries with characteristics A, B, C are likely to succeed with approach X."
